# Prologue


## Benedor


Benedor knew well the omens that would herald the fall of his empire.


The first of the three signs would be a red moon, staining  the starry night like freshly spilled blood. Then the Lifetree would ignite as its golden fruits burned with the fires of Hell. Finally, a black void would open up, and his Maker would call him home.


Long ago had he dreamt these signs and long ago had he overcome his fear of them. He was a God, after all. It was golden ichor that coursed through his veins, not the red blood of mortals.


What cause had he to feel fear?


It was true that the dream had once unnerved him. But he had bargained with the Lord of Death, and since then a thousand blood moons had passed. Never had the Lifetree burned. Never had the void opened below his feet, and never had his old master called him home.


The winds gusted, rippling the long grass at Benedor’s feet, tugging long gold curls from under his iron crown. His bearskin cloak snapped and flowed. He stood in the Evergarden of Ledin, his back to the great Lifetree at its center.


Each of the eleven gardens of Hellendor was like a haven of eternal spring, and Ledin was the greatest of them, just as its Lifetree was the greatest of its ilk. The garden was nestled in the shadow of the floating black tower of Orin. It hung high over the continent of Hellendor like an emerald studded in the snow-white clouds.


From the lofty heights of Ledin, Benedor looked down to his empire. Directly beneath this seat in the clouds was Uril, the city of a thousand towers, symbol of his holy empire’s might. Around it sprawled the continent of Hellendor, the myriad city-states bound fast by tight fist and iron throne. His Maker had demanded a dear price for this vision of peace. Benedor had paid it, and in doing so he had saved his people. It was the only way.


How fleeting the lives of mortals were, he reflected bitterly. The sum of their breaths measured but a moment against the eternity of his own life.


The Goddess’ voice came softly over his shoulder. “Will you not listen to reason, Benedor?”


He did not look back. He knew what she had come to say. He had no time to heed her advice.


It was a red moon, after all, and the Lord of Death demanded his Offerings.


“My son,” she spoke again, with more urgency. Impatiently, he turned to face her.


The Goddess floated before him, several handspan above the green grasses of the Evergarden, limned by the light of its Lifetree. She wore her Autumn form. Her golden curls drifted in a weightless halo about her face and her dress was spun of glittering red silk that danced in the gentle winds. Benedor stared deep into her eyes. So like his own they were.


“I am not your son,” he said angrily. “I care not that you Pierced the Veil. I care not that you have her memories. You are not her. Why are you here? Did Yomur send you?”


“I came to warn you,” she bit back. “One of the men tonight may kill you. Relios’ son. His profile aligns with my visions.” She looked up to red moon, her mouth twisting with distaste. “If you must kill, find another.”


“I have seen Relios’ son. He is only a boy, and he is nothing like his father. You must think me a fool, that I would lose to a child.”


“Only a fool would be so sure of victory against a foe he has not yet faced. You do not know him.”


“I have faced greater dangers.” The old scar at his wrist pulsed, a gift from the Ironhearted. The only man in a thousand years that had ever made him bleed.


“I speak of greater threats than Ifaldur,” replied the Autumn Goddess impatiently. “You will die if you fight Verrin.”


Benedor looked up to the blood moon, now nearing the peak of its arc. There was still time left to sacrifice the Offerings. But he would have to hurry, or risk the wrath of his Maker. He exhaled slowly. “Is that all you have come to say to me?”


Her gaze frosted. “If you will not heed caution, so be it. What of Rena? Would you let her die too, for your ego?”


“What is it you imply?” he asked angrily.


“I can keep her safe.”


“Do you think so little of me?” Benedor felt another flash of cold fury. He turned his back on the Goddess once again and looked out on his empire. “I have heard enough. Leave, now.”


“My son—”


“I said leave!” he roared, and a flutter of silver sparrows burst from the leaves of the Lifetree. “Once before you stole her from me. Never again. I made a vow.”


“I wished then only to save her just as I only wish to save them now. Perhaps you would see, were you not blinded by your arrogance.”


Them, she said.


“I have no need of your help.” Benedor glanced to the black tower of Orin, looming imperiously over the Evergarden. His gaze passed up the tower’s height, from the seat of his great throne to the tiers of spiked ramparts and higher still to the Tower’s crown. Rena would be safe there, he assured himself. It was folly to think otherwise.“If you take them from me,” he vowed to her darkly, “let this be the last I ever see you.”


He thought his words would hurt her. He hoped they would.


But her cold expression did not yield, as if this was all she expected of him. “A needless oath. I do not intend to live past this night.”


That gave him pause. “Will you take another form?”


“That information is not yours to know.” She turned to leave, then looked back over her shoulder. “Before that, I vow that I will save your child from you. I wonder which of our oaths Fates will choose to honor.”


And with that she left, a golden blur flying from from his tower. Benedor felt the familiar loneliness reseal his heart in iron.


He turned to the Lifetree that stood in the center of the garden. Tall it was, silver of trunk and green of leaf, its boughs heavy with glittering golden fruit. For a while he stood there, alone and sheltered under the great tree’s dense canopy. He listened—to the song of the sparrows, to the questing wind, to the steady march of his own beating heart.


Red wings flashed above, stirring Benedor from his waking dream. He blinked, looking up. There flew a lone phoenix, tracing a fiery curve across the night sky. He turned back to the black tower, watching for Seltin to arrive with the Offerings.


Soon after, the tower’s great gates swung open. From their depths walked a lone Soulmage. He seemed to glide through the long, undulating grasses, his quiet steps hidden under a long white robe. A thick gray mustache sat on his lip, his thick gray hair coiled in a loose bun atop his head and speared with a thin golden rod. In one hand he held a Staff made of living wood, as was the custom of his Order; and in the other a long blade, sheathed in a black scabbard. As he neared Benedor the Soulmage knelt. A curtain of gray hair escaped its knot to veil his face.


“Lord Emperor,” he said to the ground. His voice was quiet and cold. “The Offerings await your blade.”


Benedor looked to them now as they shuffled forth from the Black Tower. They numbered fourteen, men and women both, tethered with thick iron chains that clinked and flashed in the starlight. Their footfalls were cushioned by the long, undulating grass.


These were the Offerings chosen to be sacrificed to the Lord of Death. Benedor told himself again that it hardly mattered how they died, whether by his blade or by a Soulmage’s Staff as they were reincarnated back to the Tree, to appear again as golden fruit. But he knew better. These souls would damned by his hand. Never again would they live another life.


He knew their names, each and every one. He owed them at least that dignity.


The fourth Offering was the one of which the Goddess had warned him. Verrin was middle-aged and mean of stature, so unlike his father, Relios. Verrin’s gaze was fixed to his feet but Benedor marked the proud set to his shoulders. In that much, perhaps, he resembled the Great Bear of Feriador. But it was little cause to fear the small man.


He looked down to the Soulmage kneeling at his feet. “Rise, Seltin,” he commanded, and he reached out a hand for his greatsword.


“Yes, My Lord.” Seltin held it up, still kneeling. He did not rise until the Emperor had taken the blade. Howling Wind, the sword was named. Its steel hid in a simple black scabbard, but its hilt was work of art, wrought of gold and obsidian and studded with bright blue gems.


The blade sang as Benedor whipped it from its sheath. Perfectly balanced, he thought. Yet still it weighed heavy in his hand when he considered the task before him. He looked again to the Offerings, and his shoulders sagged.


“How much easier this would be,” Seltin mused quietly beside him, “if they would only kill themselves.”


Benedor scowled. Seltin was the last of his Order who remained loyal, so perhaps he should feel grateful for his company. Yet he hated the Soulmage in that moment, hated that his thoughts were so impure. “It would take a heartless man indeed to find killing easy.”


“Alas that such is our duty.” Seltin beckoned forth the Offerings. He arrayed them in a neat line before their Emperor.


“You know why you are here,” spoke the Soulmage.  You have been chosen. Chosen to be the noblest of sacrifices.” He looked up to the great Lifetree, then back to the Offerings. “Your souls will not live anew as they deserve. Yet in denying, they let others live.”


At that Benedor approached to the first of the Offerings. The man was old, his long gray hair matted and dark with grease, his beard patchy and unkempt. “Eldin, son of Erodin, son of Bea. Will you answer the call of the Lord of Death?”


At his words, Eldin looked up, his eyes rimmed with red, and Benedor searched them for a hint of defiance. After a while the man lowered his gaze. “I will answer,” he said solemnly.


“May He welcome you with open arms.” The Emperor’s blade flashed and drew a red line across the man’s leathery neck. Blood welled at the cut and trickled down in thick rivulets, bubbling with his dying breath. Seltin unhooked him and dragged the spent Offering’s body to the Lifetree, leaving a slick trail in the green grass.


“Go in peace,” Benedor spoke. Though he knew it would not be so.


Blood sprayed as he whipped his red blade to the side. He looked up to the Lifetree. No new fruit of gold would bloom on its boughs with Eldin’s soul. The dying Offering would have no chance at another life. Long ago, such thoughts might have weighed him with misery. But he had long since inured himself to the pain of killing.


He tried not to look back at the Soulmage. Often before he had seen a spark in Seltin’s eye on these nights of killing. He had no wish to see it again.


He faced the next Offering. She was a woman, tanned and dark of hair, her long limbs corded with slender muscle. A fisherwoman, perhaps, or a farmer of the Leja plains. When she met his gaze he saw her eyes were angry and proud. He would offer her the same choice, though he knew how she would answer.


“Tira, daughter of Une and Loray. Will you answer the call of the Lord of Death?”


“I will not,” she spat. “Not before my time.”


Seltin stepped forward and pressed a light blade into her waiting hand. Twice she spun it, weighing the balance, and Benedor saw inexperience written in her movements. He would make hers a quick death, he decided. He would make it merciful.


With an animal scream she rushed at him, sword raised in a vicious two-handed hold. He readied his own blade, setting his feet in the long grass, angling his blade slightly in the Way of Stone. He waited, patient as the timeless mountains, and as Tira’s blade began to fall toward his head, he snapped his own forward. A curtain of blood sheeted from her throat and she fell thudding to her knees. She did not resist as Seltin pulled her too to the Tree, to lie beside the first.


Benedor moved to the third woman. Linva, she was called. She cowered at his approach. “Not yet,” she begged, choking on her tears. “Please. Not yet.”


It pained him to hold her gaze, but she deserved at least that much. “Linva, daughter of Leo and Fione. Will you answer the call of the Lord of Death?”


“Your blade, Lord Emperor,” interrupted Seltin.


Benedor looked down. Tira’s blood dripped from his blade. He wiped it against his black robe, before facing Linva once more.


“I’m not ready,” she sobbed. She fell  backing away. In her panic she forgot the chain that bound her ankle, and as it snapped taut she crashed hard to the ground. Seltin moved forward to help her to her knees while Benedor waited. He hated when they cried. Could they not see there was no other way?


“I will go in her place,” spoke a low voice.


Benedor faced the next Offering. Verrin held his gaze with a fiery fury, and for a brief moment he felt a flicker of unease. He quieted the doubt. He was a God. He had no cause to fear a mere mortal. “Her soul is still owed.”


“She asks only for more time. Not to be spared.”


Benedor knew better. The woman would ask for her life to be spared. All cowards did, in the end. Yet he supposed the order of their deaths mattered little. “Very well. Verrin, son of Relios and Niruye. Will you answer the call of the Lord of Death?”


Verrin’s lips parted in a wolf’s smile. “I will not,” he said with conviction. “Perhaps you would, in my place.”


His arrogance would be his undoing. Benedor watched Seltin unclasp Verrin’s chain and offer to him a fresh blade. But Verrin waved it away with a mocking laugh. “I have no need of that.”


“You wish to fight without blades?”


“I brought my own,” Verrin replied. He made no move to draw it.


“You did not,” said Seltin. “We made sure of it.”


“Perhaps you are less thorough than you think.” Verrin eyes flashed with madness as he stared at the Emperor. “You have grown complacent. A fat spider who waits for his prey to impale itself in his fangs. No longer. I have come to cut down your web.”


Seltin stepped forward as if to shield him, but Benedor waved him back impatiently. There was nothing to fear. Not from a mortal. He readied his blade. “It is rare that I enjoy taking a life.”


“I am honored.” Verrin folded into a mocking bow. Still he showed no weapon. He walked a slow, wide arc, forcing Benedor to turn until his back was to the Tree. Then he raised his arms towards the silver stars, as if to conjure a blade from their growing flames.


Benedor blinked. The stars seemed to be growing larger. Brighter. He peered closer, careful to keep Verrin in his vision, and he saw then that they were not stars, but rather blades. A thousand of them.


They flew like a murmuration of steel sparrows, silver of beak and feather and claw, and as Benedor watched they gathered to form a single, terrible being. Wicked as death it was, with long wings tipped in fierce talons; a fat, serpentine body that tapered into a wickedly spiked tail; and deep, intelligent eyes embedded in a horned, lizard-like head. Out of habit, Benedor avoided its gaze, though it was plain this creature was not of flesh and bone and scale. No live dragon had flown for hundreds of years. Not since he first took the holy throne.


Benedor was not afraid. Not when he saw the terrible beast of steel shred through the remaining Offerings like a hurricane of deadly blades. Not when he heard the rending of flesh, the animal cries of the dying, and a grating scream of fusing metal.


No, he was not afraid. He was a God, after all. What cause had he to feel fear?


Then a flash of red, a great thunder that drowned out all other sound, and Benedor felt himself being thrown backward. For a moment, all was black.


When he woke, he saw the Lifetree burning. The second sign.


Ash rained. White flames ate acidly at the tree’s long silver trunk, shriveled and blackened its rich golden fruit until they burst with wretched screams and fell steaming to the grass.


Benedor blinked hard. Verrin stood at the edge of the Garden. Before him hovered a writhing, whirling mass of blades, now stained with blood. As he watched, the shards seemed to glow with the harsh red light of a forge fire.


His gaze wandered up, to the heavens, to the bloody moon that stained the starry empyrean like a portal to the Hell itself.


Now came the fear.


Here were all the signs. Here came his death, and with it the fall of his Empire.


Benedor felt a fool, as the arrogant often do when they face their own mortality. He was a fool for being afraid now, and he was a greater fool for not being afraid earlier, when it might have mattered. His sword lay far away in the grass, Seltin’s prone figure lay farther. And there, stalking toward him like a tiger on a wounded deer, was Verrin.


Gone were the thousand blades. In his hand Verrin wielded a great sword, nearly the length of his own proud height. Its edges writhed with silver fire.


Gods did not feel fear, Benedor thought. Nor did they kneel. He pushed himself to his feet and once more he gripped the great blade, Howling Wind. “Why now?” he choked at his attacker.


“You called yourself a God!” roared Verrin. “You called yourself our Emperor! What God cowers in fear before his enemy? What manner of Emperor does not fight for the people he claims to love?” His blade shook with his passion. “I would see you dead, for the horror you have wrought on us.”


Benedor spat, wiped golden ichor from his lips. “So. You think you will succeed where thousands failed? Where is Ifaldur?” he taunted with a cold laugh. He adjusted his grip on Howling Wind. “Is he such a coward to let a child face me in his stead?”


Verrin’s eyes flashed in anger. His spirit was imbalanced, and Benedor would exploit it. “Ifaldur is too weak, now. In his old age he walks the path of peace.”


“What matters peace, what matters war? In death, we will all face the Lord of Death. He alone will judge us.” With that Benedor sprang forward, thundering with immortal strength, and his sword crashed down like a hammer of fate towards his foe.


Lighting struck with the meeting of their blades. Just as his fear was unfamiliar, so too was this feeling, this thrill of challenge. Too long had he reigned unopposed in his throne above the world. Again he swung his blade, and when Verrin parried it with his own, Benedor could not help but laugh as he remembered what it felt like to live.


Twice more their blades clashed. Twice more Benedor crushed Verrin lower to the ground. Twice more he felt victory at the edge of his grasp. With the next swing of Howling Wind he hacked free Verrin’s sword arm from the shoulder. He flicked his blade and flung the limb far away, the weapon still gripped in its red-stained fist.


Verrin collapsed, clutching the bloody stump of his shoulder. In defeat, he seemed so small. They all did.


Benedor looked back to where Seltin’s body lay. As he watched, the Soulmage seemed to stir, and he felt a heady flash of relief. He knew the Soulmage was a God too, if a lesser one. He could not be killed.


Benedor laughed again, remember his fear earlier. “You fought well, for a mortal,” he said to Verrin’s broken body. He held the edge of Howling Wind to the bared throat of his foe. “I wish it did not have to end so. Yet what chance could you have had, against a God? We do not die as men do.”


Verrin’s made to respond, but his words were masked by a bloody cough. He dragged his remaining hand across his mouth, leaving upon it a red smear, and kept his gaze on the ground.


“Look at me as you die,” Benedor commanded. With the tip of Howling Wind he lifted the chin of his broken enemy. In the eyes of his foe the Emperor saw a focused fire, as if it took Verrin’s whole concentration just to cling to life. The man mumbled something, and Benedor leaned forward. “Speak up. Let not your last words go unheard.”


“Go to Hell, coward.” Verrin flashed a bloody, broken smile. He spat free a red tooth.


Benedor dug the tip of his blade into Verrin’s neck, drawing a trickle of red. “I offer you another chance.”


“I named you coward,” Verrin snarled, his features twisted with a deep-rooted rage. “Only a coward would damn thousands of souls to keep his throne. I know what master you serve.” Verrin hacked another rattling cough before continuing. “I know of the deal you made. I know with whom you made it. He will lift no hand to save you from our wrath.”


“You have lost,” the Emperor pronounced. He pulled back his sword and raised it high for the killing blow. “Now you will meet the Lord of Death.”


“I am not the one fated to die tonight,” said Verrin. He tilted his head back and yelled, and Benedor looked up, startled. Then a sword of silver fire fell from the heavens and arrowed into his heart, pinning him to the long grass.


He felt pain then, such as he had never known before.


“I was loyal,” he choked. He tried to look up but could not move his head.


As he looked up in agony, the black tower began to crumble. Boulders of obsidian rained about him. Then the tower stabbed down from the heavens like a dagger into the city of Uril, and Benedor looked down in horror as his white city was razed to ash and rubble.


His heart spasmed and he looked down. He was impaled on the great blade. A growing pool of golden ichor bubbled from his chest. He wondered belatedly if this indeed would be his end.


A strangled cry caught his fading attention. He watched in a daze as Verrin collapsed to the ground, his severed head rolling from his prone body. Benedor felt darkness seep into his vision. He felt a crippling fear, that he would die before seeing his wife once more.


“My lord Emperor!” called a voice, as if from a great distance.


“Rena?” Benedor’s vision strained to focus he saw Seltin kneeling by his side. Howling Wind dangled from his limp wrist, its blade stained red with Verrin’s blood.


“Alas, it is only me, my Lord Emperor. Come. Let me take you home to her.”


Benedor blinked. He turned his gaze fondly to the old Soulmage. They had been brothers once, he remembered. Before the world knew of such things as crowns and thrones.


“Go, my most loyal friend,” he spoke quietly. He coughed, wincing at the stabbing pain in his chest. “I have seen the signs. Leave me before you are forced to share my fate.”


Seltin shook his head weakly. “I will not leave you. Not like they did.” He bled from his cheek, a steady stream of ichor as gold as Benedor’s own, and his eyes were bright with tears.


Benedor recognized his fear. Who could not fear their Maker? He saw it so clearly now, his duty. He only wished he could have seen it earlier. “I will not let Him have you,” he vowed. “You will return to this world. For I need you to watch over them. Keep them safe. For me.”


“Yes, my Lord Emperor,” promised Seltin.


“I am your Emperor no longer.” Benedor clasped the old Soulmage’s hand. “I would enjoy my last moments as your brother.”


Seltin smiled and squeezed his hand. Then Benedor watched him walk toward the Lifetree. A black circle ringed its flaming silver trunk, and Seltin appeared to tap it with his Staff. “I will do as you wish, brother,” he said. Then he vanished.


“You always have,” Benedor whispered to the night.


Light bathed his face then, and he looked up, sharply. For a moment he doubted his eyes.


There in the heavens saw the Autumns Goddess, shining and gold. In her arms she carried a woman.


Rena looked peaceful in her sleep, the blue gem at her throat glimmering in the Autumn Goddess’ golden aura. And though the distance between them was great, he saw the Goddess smile at him.


So she had disobeyed his final order. He smiled. That was well.


Then the gold blur of her fleeing form arced toward the world below. The loneliness returned. He felt as if she had taken with her all that made him good.


He closed his eyes. As he lay in the ever patient night, he felt a flicker of futile hope. Perhaps he might be spared of his fate. He had served his master well, after all. Had offered so much of himself and his people. Perhaps the third sign would not come this night.


Yet his faith was not to be rewarded.


A black pit yawned open in the Garden with a belch of hot and fetid air. A portal to the very depths of Hell. And from its depths spoke an evil voice that grated like knife on stone.


“So ends your reign, Benedor, son of Yomur, son of Ea. Will you answer the call of the Lord of Death?”


“I will answer,” Benedor said quietly.


Then his body faded to dust, and he went forth to meet his master.

# Chapter 1


## The Dream


Like most unusual nights, this one began rather normally.


Kotyr meditated with Bern, watching the sun set over the glittering sea. He studied the Words that he’d speak the next day. He shared a meal with his mother, Tilia. He studied some more. It was a heavy task he faced tomorrow, and he’d be a fool not to take it seriously. Then he slept, and he dreamt.


In his dream he stood amid a beautiful, endless lea. A rainbow of flowers blanketed the waving grass and danced in the soft breeze. The sun shone warm overhead and it was serene, silent but for a brush of wind and the lilting notes of birdsong. It was Heaven, he knew.


Kotyr reached out to catch a swirl of petals. He saw the hand was not his own. Indeed he felt taller, stronger, in a strangely familiar body, and he wondered from which past life he remembered this vision. Thousands of years had passed since the realm of Heaven was lost, so it must be an old, old life. Yet the dream was clear. Almost real.


Then he noticed the dragon.


His eye registered a flicker of movement and he turned abruptly. A form began to take shape, there on the distant horizon. Its iron scales flashed as it drew nearer and he caught a glimpse of great leathery wings. It was no small beast, he saw. Not like those flightless wyrms rumored now to haunt the Western Steppes of Hellendor. No, in his dream he saw a beautiful, monstrous creature, one of scale and muscle and fire. He remembered the age-old advice and avoided its eyes, though this was but a dream.


The dragon roared as it flew toward him, and in that terrible sound was the cadence of intelligence. There were words there. Words he couldn’t understand, or perhaps didn’t wish to.


Kotyr woke in a cold tremor. Twenty quick, shallow breaths passed before he could slow his racing heart. Twenty more, slow and shaky, until he knew where he was. It was night. Cold moonlight filtered through the window. His sparse room in the Konai Temple began to take familiar shape. He had few possessions, like most Soulmages. Near the window was a desk strewn with several scrolls and a thick, dusty tome. A lute leaned against it, and in the corner sat a chest with his robes and a cloak hung on a hook near the door.


He jumped when the phoenix cried out from the Evergarden. His nerves had not yet quieted. With a sigh he rose and left, pausing on the way to grab his lute.


A storm had crept in overnight and covered the world in thick white blanket. Nestled lower on the Western slopes was the walled city of Konai, lit by the warm fire of a thousand torches. The Emperor’s banner gleamed in the East, visible even from this distance. It waved defiantly in the quiet winds.


Fifteen years had passed since Verrin had killed the Emperor. Benedor’s black tower had fell from the heavens to smother the white city beneath a shroud of ash and dust. Thousands had did, never to live another life. Kotyr’s father was but one of them.


Kotyr couldn’t help but feel a stab of regret. Maybe the loss would sting less if he knew his father, or if his mother did. But he had not yet been born, and Tilia remembered nothing from before she came to Konai. Trauma, Bern said. She’d lost so much that perhaps she chose not to remember it.


He stepped out onto the warm green grass. The Konai Evergarden was perched high in the Skybreaker mountains, like an emerald studded in their snow-white crown. On this night the moon shone bright, smiling wanly from a bed of stars.


Kotyr looked to the great Lifetree, its fruits glimmering gold in the starry night. The phoenix circled high above. After a moment’s hesitation he strode toward the two figures seated under its canopy.


Tilia, turned at his approach. His mother’s robe was the pale blue of a cloudless sky, as was the jewel set in her silver necklace. It was the only jewelry she wore. Her smile as she noticed his expression. “Are you well, Kotyr?” she asked with concern.


Kotyr sat beside her, laying his lute on the gentle grass. “I couldn’t sleep,” he whispered, not wanting to disturb Bern.


On Tilia’s other side, Bern was the very picture of peace. He sat still as stone in either a deep meditation or a deep sleep. The Soulmage’s hair and beard were tidied into clean white braids. A sharp contrast to Kotyr’s untidy mop of dark hair. Bern was draped in a plain white robe like Kotyr’s and as always, he held his great wooden Staff in his hand.


Kotyr waited in silence, watching the phoenix fly above. Allefira, Bern had named her. She had grown old, now in the twilight of her years. Her wings beat weakly, her brilliant scarlet feathers were tipped with silver, and she sang with a tremor in her voice. Yet to Kotyr she seemed beautiful as ever.


His gaze misted and he sighed wearily, letting his eyes close. He thought of how little time the phoenix had left. He thought of the role he would play in her death.


He had mastered the spells of reaping and releasing a soul. In Elisari, the language of the Gods, spells were written with a thousand twenty four words. But with practice, a mage could partition their mind into as many pieces, so they could speak the whole spell as a single, layered word. It had taken Kotyr fifteen years of study.


Tomorrow, he would reincarnate the phoenix and finally become a Soulmage.


Bern’s eyes fluttered open, his face creasing in a familiar smile. “Ah, Kotyr. Could you not find sleep?”


“I had a dream. I can’t get it out of my head.”


“A dream of what?” asked Tilia.


“I dreamed I was in Heaven,” he answered. “I dreamed I was in a sunlit field dappled with flowers, and…in the distance, was this thing.” Kotyr hesitated. “A dragon.”


Worry flashed in Bern’s eyes. “I see. A sight that would steal the sleep of even the most seasoned warrior. Creatures of pure terror. Perhaps a story, to clear you mind?”


“A lighthearted tale,” suggested Tilia. “The song of King Urthen and the Lady of the Moon.”


Bern smiled. “So many times have I told that tale that by now, you must know it as well as I do. But no doubt it would distract you.”


The phoenix Allefira’s song drifted down as she soared amongst the stars, a melody heavy with melancholy and hope. Kotyr felt another pang of anxiety. Tomorrow would bring more responsibility than he’d ever known.


“I don’t wish to be distracted from the dream.” The dream was not some errant memory of a past life, he knew. He knew it for what it was, with a certainty that surprised him. “I don’t think it came naturally. I think it was sent.”


“Sent?” Tilia’s eyes widened.


“I’m not sure,” he admitted. “To me it felt like a message.”


“A message saying what?”


Kotyr remembered the dragon and suppressed a shiver of fear. “That Heaven isn’t safe. At least not yet.”


“And who would have cause to send such a message?” prompted Bern. He had a twinkle in his eye and Kotyr felt as if he were being tested.


He considered. “Maybe it was a boast. So the Lord of Death, or one of his agents. But I don’t think so. I think it was meant as a reminder. Of our duty.” Kotyr held Bern’s gaze. “I think it was sent by the Goddess.”


“Indeed,” said the old mage, clearly pleased. “On this, I will defer to your wisdom. It was your dream, after all, and as such it should be yours to interpret as you wish. If it was sent by the Goddess, then I shall tell you a more fitting tale. Perhaps the story of when I was first given my duty, all those years ago. Will that do?”


Kotyr smiled. “I’d like that.”


“Very well.” Bern leaned against the Lifetree. “Few indeed remember this tale as I do. Where else to start, but the beginning of all things? Thousands of years ago, the people of this world lived as mere children, blissful in their naivety. Those were times of peace, of prosperity, when death itself held no meaning. For a hundred years the people lived so, and the light that shone from their souls showed no signs of waning.


“But the nature of time is to change, and like all eras this too had to end. It was on a night like this one, quiet and filled with stars, that I and my brothers fell to this World.”


“From Heaven?” asked Kotyr. “Do you remember it? Was it like I saw in my dream?”


“This story is mine to tell, and I will tell it was I wish.” Bern frowned. “I do not remember what Heaven was like,” he added after a pause.


“I shouldn’t have interrupted.” Kotyr bowed his head respectfully. “Please continue.”


The old mage looked to the Lifetree before continuing. “It was here that I emerged. The Tree burst forth from the very mountain, and as I walked, lush grass sprouted from my steps. My own humble paradise. I looked on the world in awe. Majestic peaks with crowns of white reared about me, frozen lakes and icy rivers glittered like gems on their faces. From the stubborn swathes of green pine I heard the melodies of sweet birdsong. And in the distances I marked flashes of gold, so bright and divine, and I knew I was sharing this wonder with my brothers.


“I surmised my duty might be as a shepherd, sent by the Maker and the Mother Goddess to guide this people in their ways. Indeed, that remains part of my role.


“Soon a bolt of lightning cracked before me, and when I blinked I beheld the Goddess herself. Of ice was her crown, pure white her cloak, her eyes a shock of blue. She was in her Winter form, a form she has never worn since that night. I fell trembling to my knees, so overwhelmed was I, and there I waited until she set her hand upon my head in blessing. Well do I remember her words.


“‘Bern, my son,’ she spoke, ‘I am afraid you have little cause to rejoice at my coming. I have a duty to ask of you.’ You have but to name it, I answered. She considered her words before she spoke, and I began to worry. ‘Fate waits,’ she said at last. ‘He waits for us all, and unceasing is his hunger. You must not let him have their souls. Take them, before we lose them to what waits beyond.’


“In horror, I asked if she meant for me to kill them. ‘Never,’ she forbade. ‘I ask you to save them. Save them, and let their souls live again in flesh made anew. For with this curse whose weight we must bear, I also give unto you the gift of creating life. Treasure it. Hold it dear. It is the only weapon we have left in this fight.


“I told her it would be as she wished. For what else could I say? At that she smiled. ‘My heart breaks that I must lay this burden on you. I will fight for the day when I can return again to tell you that which you will long to hear most. That Heaven is freed, and with it your duty fulfilled. I will fight, and I ask that you do the same, in this way. Until that day comes, or until you find one to take this weight from your soul, if you find you grow weary.’”


For a moment Kotyr sat in a contemplative silence. Tomorrow he would become a Soulmage in name, and with it would come the duties of the Order. Sacred duties, given to Bern by the Goddess herself, now passed on to him. He looked east and saw the horizon graying with crisp dawn. “Thank you,” he said quietly. “You’ve given me clarity.”


“I am glad to hear that is so,” said the old mage.


“I hope you’re not wearying of your burden already.”


Bern smiled. “Not for a while yet,” he said, and Kotyr breathed a sigh of relief.


The winds were laden with the scent and sound of the Sea. In their wake they left ripples in Kotyr’s long plain robe and tousled his dark waves of his hair. A leaf shook loose from the Lifetree and it floated, featherlike, to land in his outstretched palm. He felt warmth from its touch before it faded in a shimmer of golden dust.


“A song, Kotyr, if you will,” Tilia asked. She settled beside Bern against the great tree’s silver trunk.Kotyr listened to the music of the phoenix above. It reminded him of his dream of Heaven, and he wondered what hidden meaning it held. He wondered whether it was the Goddess who’d sent it. He wondered whether she would return, and what form she might take. Spring, he hoped.


Then he closed his eyes, holding his lute, and from those strings he coaxed an aimless, wandering tune.
