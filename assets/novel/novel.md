# Prologue

## A Black Moon, A Thousand Blades, and a Burning Tree

Benedor knew the three signs that would herald the fall of his empire.

He had dreamt them, long ago, and he had feared them too, for a little while after the vision. He had been afraid of the slim crescent of the moon as each month it again withered to a merest sliver. He had been afraid of the blades of those few Offerings who chose to fight for their lives, he had even banned fire itself.

Yet he was not afraid now, even as he looked to the cloudless night and saw the black moon. The first sign. He stood now on the edge of the Yural Evergarden. Vast it was, this haven of eternal spring, the greatest of the eleven of Hellendor. Just as the Lifetree stood in its center was the greatest of its ilk.

Benedor looked down on his empire, sprawled far below like a living tapestry, its patchwork Kingdoms bound and held fast with his iron hand and iron throne. Yes, he had once feared the three signs. But he had seen a thousand black moons since the dream, he had bloodied thousands of blades in offering to the Lord of All Death, and not once had the Lifetree burned.

The voice that spoke from over his shoulder was soft and steeped in a subtle melody. “Will you not heed reason?” she asked.

The winds gusted, whipping the long black curls from under his iron crown, and rippling his bearskin cloak. Still he did not turn to face her. He stood unperturbed, in solemn watch over the continent of Hellendor. Over his empire. His people.

“Benedor,” she said urgently, and finally he looked back to her. “Did you hear me?”

The Goddess’ golden curls floated in a weightless halo, her golden eyes glimmered in the starlight, and her dress, spun of a golden silk, danced with the gentle spring winds. She floated before him, a foot above the green grasses, limned by the light of the great Lifetree.

“Why are you here?” he asked. With some reluctance he lowered his gaze to meet hers. “Did Yomur send you?”

“I came of my own accord,” she spoke. “So that I might warn you before it is too late. Will you not look on him, at least, so you may judge his threat for yourself?”

He bristled at that. “You must think me a fool. Of course I have looked on him. I saw only a boy.”

“I do not think you a fool, Benedor. But only a fool would underestimate a foe he has not yet faced. You have not met Relios’ son.”

“I might have feared Ifaldur. Or Relios himself.” Benedor exhaled slowly. “You told me you are a Goddess. A Mayyi.”

He wondered if those golden eyes could see his thoughts. “I spoke the truth. My blood runs gold,” she said.

“Then I too must be Mayyi.” He held her gaze, and hers did not waver. “Gods do not bleed.”

“Not by the hands of ordinary men, perhaps.”

Benedor did not answer. He looked up to the black moon, as if to reassure himself he no longer feared the omen. “Is that all you have come to say to me?”

“No.” She sighed. “If you will not heed caution, so be it. Would you risk your wife too? For your ego?”

“You dare?” he asked quietly.

“I only offer, my son. I can keep her safe.”

“You will not take her from me. Not again.”

“Benedor—”

“I said no!” he shouted, and a flutter of silver sparrows burst from the leaves of the Lifetree. He dropped his gaze. “I said no,” he repeated. “You stole her from me once already.”

“I wished only to save her.” She held his gaze. “As I wish only to save them now.”

Them. So she knew.

Benedor’s gaze drifted to the Black Tower that loomed imperiously over the Evergarden. This was the symbol of his Holy Empire. He gaze passed up the Tower’s height, from the seat of his iron throne, to the tiers of spiked ramparts that housed a fleet of archers, and higher still to the Tower’s crown. There his was safe, he knew. It was folly to think otherwise. His lip curled, that the Goddess pitied him so, that even now she plotted for what was rightfully his. “If you take them from me,” he said darkly, “let this be the last I ever see you.”

He thought his words would hurt her. Part of him hoped they would, even as another part hated himself for thinking so.

She offered him a sad smile, as if this was all she expected of him. “Yet I hope to see you again, my son. We will see which of our wishes the Fates choose to honor.” And as she left, a distant golden blur arcing away from his Evergarden, he felt the familiar loneliness encase his heart in iron.

He turned now to the giant that stood in the center of the garden. Tall it was, silver of trunk and green of leaf, and from its web of boughs hung bunches of golden fruits. For a while he stood there, alone under the Lifetree’s dense canopy. He listened to the song of the silver sparrows, to the steady march of his beating heart, to his thoughts as they flickered in and out of awareness.

A flash of red above stirred Benedor from his waking dream. He blinked, peering up the red phoenix as it traced fiery curves in the night sky, watching it spar with the silver sparrows that nested in the Lifetree. He looked to the Black Tower and glimpsed a dim dark hall as the gates slowly swung open.

From the Tower swept a Sage and he seemed to glide across the long, undulating grasses, his quiet steps hidden under a long white robe. Above his lip sat a thick gray mustache, and his long gray hair was coiled in a loose bun atop his head and speared with a thin gold rod. In one hand he held a Staff made of living wood, and in the there a long blade sheathed in a long black scabbard. As he neared Benedor he knelt, and a curtain of hair escaped its knot to veil his face.

“Lord Emperor,” the Sage said to the ground. His voice was quiet and cold. “The Offerings await your blade.”

The Offerings. Benedor looked to them now. Eleven of them there were, men and women both. They shuffled forth from the Black Tower, iron chains clinking and flashing in the starlight, footfalls cushioned by the long, undulating grass, and their faint wordless cries carried forward by the wind. Here and there one stumbled, only to be lifted to their feet by their comrades in chains.

The fourth Offering was the one of which the Goddess had warned him. Verrin was middle-aged and mean of stature, so unlike his father—Lord Relios, the Great Bear of Feriador. Verrin’s gaze was fixed to his feet but Benedor noticed a proud set to his shoulders. In that much, perhaps, he resembled the Great Bear. But there seemed little reason to fear him.

He looked down to the kneeling Sage. “Rise, Seltin,” he commanded, and he reached out a hand for his greatsword.

“Yes, My Lord.” Seltin proffered it to him as he knelt, and he did not rise until the Emperor had taken it. Howling Wind, the sword was called, and though the blade hid sheathed in a simple black scabbard, the hilt betrayed its craftsmanship—worked of gold and obsidian and studded with rubies the color of blood.

Steel sang as Benedor whipped it from its sheath. Perfectly balanced. Yet still it weighed heavy in his hand when he considered the task before him.

It had been one thing to cull a single man each cycle. He looked again to the eleven Offerings, and his shoulders seemed to sag, as if with the weight of each of their souls.

“How much easier this would be,” mused the Sage quietly, “if they would only kill themselves.”

Benedor’s eyes flashed. “It would take a heartless man indeed to find killing easy.”

“Alas that such is our duty,” replied Seltin, unmoved.

Never before had the old Sage seemed so cruel to Benedor as he did in that moment. Seltin turned away sharply to beckon forth the Offerings, arraying them in a line before their Emperor.

“You know why you are here,” said Benedor. “You have been chosen, chosen to be the noblest of sacrifices. Your souls will not live anew as they deserve.” He glanced up to the great Lifetree, then back to the Offerings. “Yet in death, you let the others of this world live to see another moon. I will offer each of you the same choice.”

At that he approached to the first of the Offerings. The man was old, his long gray hair matted and dark with grease, his beard patchy and unkempt. “Eldin, son of Erodin, son of Bea,” pronounced the Emperor. “Will you answer the call of the Lord of All Death?”

At Benedor’s words, Eldin looked up, his eyes rimmed with red, and Benedor searched them for a hint of defiance. After a while the man lowered his gaze. “I will answer His call,” he answered solemnly.

“May He welcome you with open arms.” The Emperor’s blade flashed and drew a red line across the man’s leathery neck.

“Go in peace,” Benedor spoke, though he knew he would not.

Seltin dragged the spent Offering’s body to the Lifetree, leaving a slick trail in the green grass. Benedor whipped his red blade to the side and the blood sprayed free.

Benedor looked up to the Lifetree. No new fruit of gold would bloom on its boughs with Eldin’s soul. The dying Offering would have no chance at another life. He would never see Hell, or Heaven, if it could become so again. Long ago, such thoughts might have weighed him with misery. But he had long since inured himself to the pain of killing. Perhaps Seltin was right. It was his duty, and doing it did not make him cruel.

He tried not to look back at the Sage. Seltin may claim to be numb, but Benedor had seen a spark in his eye when blood was in the air. He had no wish to see it again.

She was a woman, tanned and dark of hair, her long limbs corded with slender muscle. A fisherwoman, perhaps, or a farmer of the Leja plains. He offered her the same choice. When she met his gaze, angry and proud, he had no doubt of her answer.

“Tira, daughter of Une and Loray. Will you answer the call of the Lord of All Death?”

“I will not,” she spat. “Not before my time.”

Seltin stepped forward and pressed a light blade into her waiting hand. Twice she spun it, weighing the balance, but when she tried a practice stroke, Benedor pitied her in experience. He would make her death quick, he decided. He would make it merciful.

With an animal scream she rushed at him, sword raised in a vicious two-handed hold. He readied his own blade, setting his feet in the long grass, angling his blade slightly in the Way of Stone. He waited, patient as the Feriador Mountains, and as the Tira’s blade began to fall toward his head, he snapped his own forward. A curtain of blood sheeted from her suddenly split throat. She thudded to her knees. did not resist as Seltin pulled her too to the Tree, to lie beside the first.

Benedor moved to the third woman. Linva, she was called. She cowered at his approach. “Not yet,” she begged, choking on the tears that accompanied her word. “Please. Not yet.”

It pained him to hold her gaze, but she deserved at least that dignity. “Linva, daughter of Leo and Fione. Will you answer the call of the Lord of All Death?”

“Lord Emperor,” interrupted Seltin.

Benedor turned back. There was no pity in Seltin’s eyes, no remorse, just a cold sense of duty. It unnerved him, and he suppressed a shudder.

Seltin nodded to his sword and Benedor looked down. Tira’s blood dripped from his blade. He whipped it clean once more, then faced Linva.

She had begun to cry in earnest now. “Not yet,” she sobbed, backing away. In her panic she forgot her chain, and she crashed hard to the ground when it snapped taut. Seltin moved forward to help her to her knees and Benedor waited. He hated when they cried. Could they not see there was no other way?

“I will go in her place,” spoke a low voice.

Benedor looked to the next Offering. Verrin held his gaze with a fiery fury, and felt for a moment a flicker of doubt at his judgment of the man. “Her soul is still owed.”

“She asks only for more time,” Verrin snapped. “Not to be spared.”

Benedor knew better. The woman would ask to be spared next. Yet it mattered not to him, the order of their deaths. “Very well,” he spoke. “Verrin, son of Relios and Niruye. Will you answer the call of the Lord of All Death?”

Verrin’s lips parted in a wolf’s smile. “I will not,” he said, and his voice held no doubt. “

Once more, Seltin stepped forward with a fresh blade. But Verrin waved it away with a mocking laugh. “I have no need of that.”

“You wish to fight without blades?” asked Benedor.

“I brought my own,” he replied, though he made no move to draw any.

Benedor felt a sudden chill. “You did not. We made sure of it.”

“Perhaps you are less thorough than you think.” Verrin still held his smile. “Perhaps you have grown complacent. A fat spider, waiting for your prey to impale itself in your fangs. No longer. I have come to cut down your web.”

Seltin stepped forward to shield the Emperor, but Benedor waved him back. He saw no threat. “It is rare that I enjoy taking a life,” he said, as he readied his blade.

“I am honored.” Verrin folded into a mocking bow. He walked a slow, wide arc, forcing Benedor to turn until his back was to the Tree. Still the man held no weapon. He raised his arms towards the silver stars as if to conjure one from their growing flames.

Benedor blinked. Yes, he saw, the stars were getting larger. He peered closer, careful to keep Verrin in his vision, and he saw then that they were not stars, but rather blades. A thousand of them.

The second sign.

They flew like a murmuration of silver sparrows, steel of beak and feather and claw, and as he watched they seemed to form a single, terrible being.

Wicked as death it was, long wings tipped with fierce talons; a fat, serpentine body that tapered into a wickedly spiked tail; and a deep, intelligent eyes embedded in a lizard-like head. Out of habit, he avoided its gaze, though it was plain this was not of flesh and bone and scale. No live dragon had flown for hundreds of years, not since Benedor first took the Throne.

For some while, he was not afraid. He was not afraid when he saw the terrible beast of steel shred through the chained Offerings like a hurricane of deadly blades. He was not afraid when he heard the rending of flesh, the animal screams of the dying, and a grating scream of fusing metal.

No, he was not afraid. He was a Mayyi, after all. Gods did not bleed.

Then a flash of red, a great thunder that drowned out all other sound, and Benedor felt himself being thrown backward. For a moment of respite he felt nothing at all.

He woke to see the Lifetree burning.

White flames withered the long silver trunk, shriveled and blackened the rich golden fruit, burst them with wretched screams and let them fall steaming to the grass. Ash rained.

Verrin stood at the edge of the Garden. Before him hovered a writhing, whirling mass of blades, now stained with blood, aglow with a harsh red light.

Benedor’s gaze wandered up, up to the heavens, up to the black moon that stained the starry empyrean like a portal to the Void itself.

Now came the fear.

His sword lay far away in the grass, Seltin’s prone figure lay farther. And there, stalking forward like a tiger to a wounded deer, was Verrin. Gone were the thousand blades. In his hand he wielded a great sword, nearly the length of his own proud stature, its edges writhing with silver fire.

Here then were all the signs. Here came his death, and with it the fall of his Empire.

Benedor felt a fool, as the arrogant often do when they face their own mortality. He felt a fool for being afraid now, just as he felt a fool for not being afraid earlier. And still Verrin approached.

Gods did not bleed, Benedor thought from his knees. Yet neither did they cower before a fight.

He pushed himself to his feet, and once more he gripped the great blade Howling Wind. “Why now?”

“You call yourself a God? You call yourself our Emperor? What manner of God cowers in fear before his enemy? What manner of Emperor does not fight for the people he claims to love?”

“So it is to be you,” he taunted. “I always thought this to be Ifaldur’s fate.”

Verrin laughed. “He is too weak. In his old age he walks the path of peace.”

“What matters peace, what matters war? In death, we will all face the Lord of All Death. He alone can judge us.” With that Benedor sprang forward, thundering with immortal strength, and his blade Howling Wind crashed down like a hammer of death towards his foe.

Lighting struck with the meeting of their blades. Just as his fear was unfamiliar, so too was this feeling, this thrill of challenge. Too long had the Emperor sat unopposed in his throne above the world. Again he swung his blade, and when Verrin parried it with his own, Benedor could not help but laugh. He remembered what it felt to live.

Twice more their blades clashed, twice more Benedor crushed Verrin lower to the ground, twice more he felt victory at the edge of his grasp. With the next swing of Howling Wind he hewed loose Verrin’s sword arm from the shoulder. He flicked his blade and flung far away the limb and the weapon it still gripped.

Verrin collapsed, bleeding, clutching the stump of his shoulder. In defeat, he seemed so small.

Benedor looked back hastily to where Seltin’s body lay. As he watched, the Sage seemed to stir, and he felt a heady flash of relief. He knew the Sage was Mayyi, knew he could not be killed, yet even still he worried for his friend.

He turned back to Verrin’s broken body. “You fought well, for what you are. I wish it did not have to end so.” Benedor held the edge of Howling Wind to the bared throat of his foe. “Yet what chance could you have had, against a God? We do not die as men do.”

Verrin’s words were smothered by a bloody cough. He dragged his remaining hand across his mouth, leaving upon it a red smear, and kept his gaze on the ground.

“Look at me as you die,” Benedor commanded. With the tip of Howling Wind he lifted the chin of his broken enemy. In his eyes the Emperor saw a quiet fire of focus, as if it took Verrin’s whole concentration just to cling to life. He mumbled something, and Benedor leaned forward. “Speak up. Let not your last words go unheard.”

Verrin bared his bloody teeth in a mockery of a smile. “Coward,” he spat.

Benedor dug the tip of his blade into Verrin’s neck, drawing a red bead of blood. “I offer you another chance.”

“Only a coward would damn thousands of souls to keep his throne,” Verrin snarled, his features twisted with a deep-rooted rage.

Benedor chilled. “I will forgive your words, since you know not what you speak.”

“I know what master you serve.” Verrin hacked another rattling cough before continuing. “I know of the deal you made, inked in blood. He will lift no hand to save you from us.”

“You have lost,” the Emperor pronounced. He pulled back his sword and prepared the killing blow.

“I am not the one fated to die tonight,” said Verrin. He yelled, and Benedor looked up, startled, as a sword of silver fire fell from the heavens and arrowed into his heart.

He felt himself crash into the base of his Black Tower. Obsidian rained about him. Then the Black Tower fell, and like a dagger it stabbed into the city of Uril, and Benedor watched in horror as the white City was razed to ash and rubble.

His heart spasmed and he looked down, saw the growing pool of golden ichor bubbling from his chest, the great blade embedded therein.

A strangled cry brought his gaze across to Verrin’s kneeling figure. He watched in a daze as Verrin collapsed to the ground, his severed head rolling from his prone body. Benedor felt darkness seep into his vision, and for a moment he feared death, feared dying without seeing his wife once more.

“My lord Emperor!” called a voice, as if from a great distance.

“Rena?” Benedor blinked and saw Seltin kneeling by his side. Howling Wind’s blade dangled from his limp wrist, red with Verrin’s blood.

“Alas, only me, my Lord Emperor. Let me take you home to your wife.”

He looked fondly to the old Sage. They had been brothers once, he remembered. Before the world knew of such things as crowns and thrones. “Go, old friend,” he spoke quietly. “I have seen the signs. Leave me, before you are forced to share my fate.”

Seltin shook his head weakly. He bled still, a steady stream of ichor as gold as Benedor’s own. “I won’t leave you. Not like they did.”

Benedor then recognized the fear in his brother’s eyes. “I vow I will not let Him have you,” he consoled. He saw it so clearly now, his duty, and he wondered how he could not have seen it for so long. “You will return to this world, for I need you to watch over them. To keep them safe.”

“Yes, my Lord Emperor,” promised Seltin.

“No.” Benedor clasped the old Sage’s hand. “I am Emperor no longer. I would enjoy my last moments as your brother.”

Seltin smiled, squeezed his hand before walking toward the Lifetree. A black circle marred its flaming silver trunk, and Seltin appeared to tap it with his Staff. “I will do as you wish, brother,” he said.

“You always have,” Benedor spoke quietly, though the Sage had disappeared.

Light bathed Benedor’s face then, and he looked up, sharply. For a moment he doubted his eyes. There in the heavens saw the Goddess, shining and gold.

In her arms she carried a woman.

Regal and beautiful she was, peaceful in her sleep, the blue gem at her throat glimmering in the Goddess’ golden aura. Benedor could not help but smile then, sad and thankful as he was. And though the distance between them was great, he saw the Goddess smile back. Then her gold blur descended to the world and he felt as if she took with her all that had made him good.

Once more he was alone. He closed his eyes, laying in the ever patient night, and he felt a flicker of futile hope. Perhaps he might be spared of his fate. He had served well, after all. Had offered so much of himself and his people.

Yet his faith was not to be rewarded. Soon a black pit yawned open in the Garden with a belch of hot and fetid air, a portal to the very depths of Hell. And from its depths spoke an evil voice that grated like knife on stone.

“So ends your reign, Benedor, son of Yomur, son of . Your Master calls you home. Will you answer the call of the Lord of All Death?”

“I will answer,” Benedor said quietly. Still he did not open his eyes, for there was nothing more he wished to see in this world.

# Chapter 1

## The Dream

Kotyr dreamed he was in Heaven.

He stood amid an endless lea, in the unfamiliar form of a past life. Serene it was, and beautiful too. A rainbow of flowers, here bright red, there lilac, there a royal blue, splashed the undulating sea of grass. Petals floated, carried by a gentle breeze, and the sun shone bright and warm above.

It was empty, he noted. Not another soul in sight. He turned abruptly and his eye caught a flicker of movement on the distant horizon. A form there began to take shape. Its iron scales flashed as it drew nearer and Kotyr saw a hint of leathery wings.

It was a dragon.

Not a small beast, either, not like those flightless wyrms rumored to haunt the Western Steppes of Hellendor. In his dream Kotyr saw a monster of scale and muscle and fire. It roared as it flew toward him, and within that terrible sound he could hear the cadence of language.

He woke in a cold tremor. For a moment he knew not where he was, and his breath came quick and shallow until his room in the Konai temple slowly took familiar shape. It was night, yet light the Lifetree from the Evergarden flooded in, to gild the room.

Like most unusual nights, this one had begun rather normally. He’d meditated with Bern as the sun set over the sea, he’d dined with his mother, he’d studied the Words that he would speak on the morrow. He’d slept, and he’d dreamt.

Already the dream began to fade from his memory, and with a sigh he let it go.  He knew it would not do to dwell on such things. With a sigh he rose from bed, pausing to grab his lute before. stepping out into the Evergarden.

The Konai Evergarden was perched high in the Skybreaker mountains, like an emerald studded in their snow-white crown. Below, nestled lower on the Western slopes, squatted the walled city of Konai. Further West lay the twin isles of Illira and Ellora, mere specks of green floating in the starlit sea. To the South, the glimmer of the Evenstar tower of Feriador; to the North, the city of Karthax; and to the East, the dead land of Uril.

Fifteen years had passed since Verrin had killed the Emperor. Benedor’s black tower had dropped like a dagger from the heavens to smother the white city beneath a shroud of ash and dust. Yet still his silver banner gleamed from the broken tower, waving defiantly in the quiet winds.

The moon was bright that night, smiling wanly from a bed of stars, and a phoenix circled high above the Evergarden. Kotyr looked to the great Lifetree, its fruits gleaming gold in the starry night. He saw two figures seated under its canopy. After a moment’s hesitation, he strode toward them.

His mother, Tilia, turned at his approach, her smile tinged with concern. She wore a pale blue robe, sewn of the same hue as the jewel set in her necklace, the only jewelry she wore. “Kotyr,” she whispered. “Are you well?”

Kotyr sat beside her. “I couldn’t sleep.”

On Tilia’s other side, Bern was the picture of peace and stillness as sat in meditation. The Sage’s hair and beard were tidied into clean white braids which marked a sharp contrast to Kotyr’s untidy mop of dark hair. He was draped in a plain white robe, as was the custom of Sages, and he held his great wooden Staff in his hand, as he always did.

Kotyr sat in silence as he waited, watching the phoenix fly above. Allefira, she was named, and she was now grown old. In the twilight of her years, her wings beat weakly, her brilliant scarlet feathers were tipped with silver, and as she sang, Kotyr heard a tremor in her voice. Yet to him she seemed beautiful as ever.

His gaze misted and he sighed, letting his eyes close. He thought of how little time the phoenix had left. He thought of the role he would play in her death.

On the morrow he would reincarnate the phoenix, and become a Sage. He had finally mastered the spells of reaping and releasing a soul. Those spells, written in Elisari, the language of the Gods, had started as a thousand twenty four words each. But with practice, a Sage could partition their mind into that many pieces so they could speak the whole spell as a single, layered word. It had taken Kotyr fifteen years as Bern’s apprentice to achieve that mastery.

“Bad dream?” prompted Bern, as his thoughts returned to the present. The Sage’s face creased in a familiar smile when Kotyr met his gaze.

Kotyr knew what Bern thought of dreams, and he had no wish to have that conversation again. “Yes,” he said, knowing the question that would follow.

“Do you remember anything of it?” From Bern’s piercing gaze, Kotyr could tell he already knew the answer.

He could lie. On the morrow he would swear his oath of truth, after all, and this could well be his last chance. “Yes,” he answered instead. He sighed and adjusted the tension of his lute.“I can’t get it out of my head.”

He braced for a lecture, but Bern surprised him. “Of what did you dream?”

Kotyr turned and met his master’s gaze. “I dreamed I was in Heaven,” he said. “I dreamed I was in a sunlit field dappled with flowers, and…and in the distance, was this thing. A dragon.”

A flash of recognition in the Sage’s eye at the last word. Kotyr considered pressing but thought better of it.

“It has been many hundreds of years since we lost Heaven. Dreams from so long ago tend to distort with time and so lose any semblance of truth. I do not expect you to simply forget what you saw. Not even I could do such a thing. Simply remember it has no meaning in your present life.”

“Yes, master,” said Kotyr. “I know the dream meant nothing,” he added, and this time he lied.

The winds were laden with the scent and sound of the Sea. In their wake they left ripples in his long plain robe, in his dark wavy hair. A leaf shook loose from the Lifetree and it floated, featherlike, to land in his outstretched palm. He felt warmth from its touch before the leaf faded in a shimmer of golden dust.

“A song, Kotyr, if you will,” Tilia asked. She closed her eyes, settling back against the Lifetree.Kotyr looked up to the constellations—the Northern Bear, the Fisherwoman, the Huntsman and the Hound. He listened to the music of the phoenix flying above and he held his lute. Then he closed his eyes, and from those strings he coaxed an aimless, wandering tune.

# Chapter 2

## The Ceremony

The next day, Kotyr meditated in the Konai Evergarden with his back against a great Lifetree. His eyes were open, tracking the phoenix that traced a fiery blur across the evening sky.

Kotyr opened his eyes. “Allefira,” he called. The phoenix circled once, twice, then tucked into a swooping dive to alight on his shoulder. “I will miss you,” he said quietly. She let him stroke the crest of red feathers atop her head.

“This will not be her end,” spoke Bern’s voice from behind, gently but chiding. “She will be reborn again.”

“I know,” said Kotyr to the old Sage. “But she won’t be the same.”

Bern sat down beside Kotyr. Allefira hopped from Kotyr’s shoulder to his. “You speak as though that saddens you.”

“Shouldn’t it? It’s a sad loss.”

To the west, the sun burned red as it dove toward the glittering sea. Tall, ship-like clouds sailed overhead, their hulls limned in a wash of gold.

“If it is sad,” said Bern quietly, “it is a selfish flavor of grief. What you mourn is not the loss of her soul. Her soul will rest briefly at the end of a meaningful journey, and soon it will rise again. Just as the sun will, with the warmth of a rosy fire, and it will be beautiful. No, what you mourn is your own loss. Do not be so quick to relegate her life to a memory. Not while her soul lives on.”

The phoenix also watched the sun setting, though soon she turned to face Kotyr, as if she felt the weight of his gaze. She chirped inquisitively, and in the inky pools of her eyes he saw a smile twinkling. He imagined those eyes in a new body, a new life, and he felt in his soul that Bern was right. It would indeed be a beautiful thing.

Like a seed in fresh soil he could feel the Sage’s words take root. He’d have to take care to nurture that understanding, he thought. “Thank you,” he said to his master. “You’re right, of course.”

Bern smiled. “I often am. But it is I that should thank you, Kotyr.” He stood, and the wind rippled the long cloth of his cloak. “Are you ready?”

Kotyr rose too. “I am,” he answered truthfully. “This is no small task.”

“It would lose some of its significance, I think, if it were. But it is good you recognize its importance. That means you will take it seriously.” Bern smiled. “Worry not. I am here, if something were to go amiss.”

He nodded, and Kotyr reached up to the Tree of Life and grabbed a low, drooping branch. He broke it, the branch snapping with a sharp crack. The wand-shaped stick he held thrummed with latent power, and his hand shook slightly as he offered it to his master for inspection.

Bern leaned forward. He pulled out a rough cloth from within his white robe and ran it down the length of the wood to smooth its surface. Then, apparently satisfied, he stepped back and tapped it with his own great, gnarled staff. “Anondir,” he said. There was a ringing echo like that of a stone dropped into a still pond. Then a bright flash of white light, which dissolved into white mist.

“It is a fine wand,” said the Sage with pride. “And it will grow into a fine Staff in the years to come.”

Bern’s own towering staff was at least ten times the length of Kotyr’s little wand. It would take perhaps a hundred years before his grew to be that length, a hundred years in service as a Sage. He felt restless for a moment, imagining a solitary life here, and suddenly his wand felt very heavy in his hands. He shook the feeling off. “Thank you, master,” he said.

Bern nudged the phoenix, and Allefira flapped from his shoulder to Kotyr’s outstretched wrist. Her talons circled his wrist, yet she was careful not to draw blood. She blinked up at Kotyr expectantly. He laid a hand on her brow, brushing the scarlet feathers of her crested head. “Ilofilen nir alina aetheron,” he whispered. Health and prosperity in the next life.

Kotyr strode toward the Tree and rapped his new wand sharply against it, sending a wave of light rippling up its trunk.  Then he spoke the words of releasing, all thousand twenty four at once tumbling from his mouth in an alien harmony. He felt his feet leave the ground.

He floated there a moment, weightless, and could not help but laugh at the feeling. He reached his arm out toward the Tree and a moment later, a plump golden fruit fell into his outstretched hand with a satisfying plop. It felt ripe and heavy in his hand. Heavier than a fruit its size had any right to be. He floated back to the ground, landing gently on the grass. “Will that happen every spell I use, now?”

“You will learn to control it,” answered Bern, smiling. “Though it will take practice.”

“I will gladly practice.” Too soon, he felt himself drift back to the ground. Allefira joined him, gliding to the grass.

“She is ready when you are,” said Bern.

This was the moment where Kotyr would become a Sage. The moment his master had spent the past fifteen years preparing him for. After this, there would be no turning from this destiny.

Kotyr knelt before the old phoenix, offering the golden fruit, and she ate of it gladly.

Then he spoke the thousand words of reaping. Once more he rose into the evening sky, and he looked down in wonder.

A faint glowing aura emanated from the phoenix, shrouding her in a white mist that pulsed with her slowing heartbeat. Kotyr felt his wand vibrating with unchecked power and he fought to keep it steady, arm tense with exertion as he levitated with the power of the word.

“Not with your body,” chided Bern. “Physical strength is not enough to hold a soul. You will need your body, mind, and soul aligned. Start with your mind. Calm your thoughts, let them wash through you.”

The staff still shook in Kotyr’s hands, but buried under its frantic energy he felt a faint pulse, beating in a regular rhythm. He took a deep breath. He felt his own heartbeat racing in his chest and took another breath, steadier this time, forcing his to slow. The staff quieted ever so slightly in response.

“Very good,” said Bern.

Kotyr felt it then, the alignment the Sage had spoken of. A subtle awareness of all around him. A harmonic resonance deep within, a peace that rippled outward along a web of connected souls, with him at its center.

There was a blinding flash of white light. Kotyr blinked away the sting of tears, dropping slowly back to alight in the garden.

When his eyes adjusted, he saw before him an egg in a bed of ash. It was small, no larger than a raven’s, but its shell had the hue of fire.

There he swore his oath of truth, under a blanket of glittering stars with his master as witness, and when he spoke the vow he felt its weight settle in his shoulders.

Then he sat vigil and he watched the fiery egg. He was weary, but the temptation of sleep did not cross his mind. He prayed, and beside him Bern and Tilia did the same. They prayed o Rena, the Goddess of Eternal Spring, to Yomur, the Great Architect, even to the Lord of All Death. He prayed, and he waited, and he wondered what he would name the new phoenix.

Soon the eastern horizon grayed with dawn. And as the sun rose and the sky bloomed with rosy dawn, a crack appeared in the phoenix egg.

The crack spiderwebbed outward, accompanied by a muffled pecking. Then the shell collapsed, and the hatchling emerged in a snowy bed of white ash.

She was perfect, thought Kotyr. Her plumage was painted with the palette of a fiery sunset; yellow at the roots, a pastel orange burning through the middle, and a crescendo of vivid scarlet at the tips. She blinked feebly and turned her gaze up towards Kotyr. He thought he caught a flash of recognition in her intelligent eyes, but it quickly passed.

“She’s beautiful,” he said.

“Indeed she is,” said Bern. Kotyr didn’t need to look up to see the pride with which he spoke. But he did anyway, and he couldn’t help but mirror his master’s smile. “You did well, Kotyr.”

“Do you really think so?” he asked, if only to hear the compliment once more. His master offered them sparingly, and the praise thus meant a great deal.

“I do,” said Bern, with a knowing smile. He seemed to be in a generous mood. “She will need a new name. Perhaps you might do her the honor?”

“Auriella,” answered Kotyr, surprised at how sure he felt.

“A second chance,” Bern translated. “A fitting name.”

Kotyr held his hand out and the little phoenix hopped atop it, chirping happily. He cupped her hand and pulled her close to admire her more closely. “When will I have my own phoenix, Uncle?”

“One does not own a phoenix, Kotyr,” chided Bern. “But one will come to you, when you are ready.”

Kotyr hoped his disappointment was not apparent. “I’d hoped I’d be ready now,” he said, stroking Auriella’s crested head.

Bern smiled. “A hope ill-placed. You have much to learn still, Kotyr.”

At that Tilia exited the temple, sweeping across the long grass, her silken blue robe rippling in the quiet breeze. As she passed the Tree of Life she clasped her hands and closed her eyes in a brief, silent prayer.

“Good morning, Tilia,” said Bern.

“Good morning, mother,” said Kotyr.

Tilia smiled. “Hello Kotyr. Well? Are you a Sage now?”

“I am,” Kotyr replied. He held forth his hand and showed her the hatchling.

His mother gasped and bent forward, holding out her hand, and the little phoenix chirped and hopped into her outstretched palm. Tilia drew her close to her eyes, that she might see her better. “She’s so beautiful.”

Bern nodded in approval. “I said as much already. This was well done, Kotyr. I am proud of you.”

“As am I,” added Tilia. “So too would your father.”

“Thank you,” replied Kotyr. In truth, the compliment held little meaning, as she remembered nothing of his father. She remembered nothing before she came to on the door of the Konai Temple. She knew nothing of his father, and so neither did he, and that hurt more than he cared to admit.

“I mean it,” she said, as if she could tell his mind. “You are his son. How could he not feel pride when looking on you?”

Kotyr smiled, brushed away a silent tear. “All I am, I owe to you both,” he said.

They sat there through the morning, the three of them and the phoenix. And as the pale sun rose, ushering in a cloudless blue sky, Kotyr found his wand did not weigh quite as heavy as it had before.

# Chapter 3

## The Cycle of the Soul

Time settled into a gentle rhythm. A moon passed since Kotyr had performed his rites, then another moon. Each day brought a steady trickle of pilgrims to the Lifetree—in the evenings, they who came to die and add to its number of fruits, and in the mornings, they who came to eat of those fruits, so their lives may be born anew.

It was a cold morning when the Godbreaker arrived to Konai.

Dawn brought few pilgrims that day. Kotyr waited for them in the Evergarden, the bitter winds screaming and biting, and he pulled his robe tighter. To little avail.

By now he had helped reincarnate nearly twenty souls. Each time he spoke the Words of Reaping and Releasing, his wand had grown in length, and he now felt comfortable calling it a Staff. Bern had seemed proud, too, at times even eager to let him do his duty alone. Today was one such day. Bern was probably sitting in the Temple by the fire, thought Kotyr bitterly.

There were two pilgrims this morning, along with those who accompanied them. The first was a family, a middle-aged woman, along with her husband and two children. She and her husband both were tall, their profiles made bigger by the oversized cloaks draped across their shoulders. Hers was fox fur, his a great wolf.

“We have come to eat of the fruit,” the man said solemnly.

Nobility, Kotyr thought, on hearing his accent. He knew little of the politics of Konai, but he looked again at the wolf fur he wore, and was reminded of Gerion, the White Wolf, king of Konai. This was not the Prince Roshan, rather a distant cousin or uncle.

Kotyr bowed as low as etiquette demanded. The man looked pleased, in his own haughty way. “May I know who asks?”

“I am Lord Eurion. This is my wife, Lady Eda. I would know your name as well. Are you not rather young, to be a Sage?” Beside him, Lady Eda offered an apologetic smile.

“I’m called Kotyr. And I assure you I am a Sage, in more than just name.”

“There will be no need of that,” said Eda.

Kotyr turned toward the Lifetree and rapped its silver trunk sharply with his Staff. He spoke the Words of Releasing. As he did so, he felt his Staff hum, and he heard the crackle of wood as its length grew, for a moment glowing gold. He held out his hand, and a gold fruit dropped into his palm.

He bowed again as he offered Eda the fruit. “You may eat of it here, or elsewhere if you so desire.”

“I am in your debt,” she said.

“You owe no debt. I only do my duty.”

She smiled, Eurion tucking his arm around her as they walked to the edge of the Evergarden, where stairs to the walled city were carved into the mountain face.

The next couple stepped forth. Both were clad in sheepskin cloaks, simple yet seemingly warm, enough to make Kotyr feel a pang of jealousy. The woman was slight, young, with tan skin and dark hair bound in a simple braid. The man with her had the build of a great oak, and he seemed to be her husband. In his arms he carried a heavy bundle.

“I have come to eat of the fruit,” spoke the woman.

“May I know who asks?”

“I am called Nuwe. This is my husband, Orin. We seek to have a child.”

Kotyr nodded. Once more he tapped the Lifetree with his Staff, once more he spoke the Words of Releasing, once more he offered a golden fruit. She took it gratefully, and ate it before him. Her body seemed to glow after, and even when it faded Kotyr thought he could make out a slight aura.

The couple lingered, hesitating. “We have brought you a gift,” said the man, his voice rough and worn. He held forth the bundle and bowed.

“I can’t accept payment for my duty,” said Kotyr, hiding his curiosity.

“It is not payment. Only an offering.” Orin kept the bundle proffered. He did not rise.

After a moment, Kotyr accepted. He unwrapped the package and could not help but smile when he saw what was within.

“A cloak,” he said admiringly, as he held it out. He saw beneath it was another, still folded within the bundle.

“We are but simple shepherds,” said Nuwe. “I regret this is all we have to offer.”

“It’s a most generous gift. I thank you.” Kotyr tried the cloak on gratefully, relishing the warmth. The sheepskin was thick and soft, and though the wind still bit his face and hands, he found it more bearable. As the couple departed, he entered the Temple with the other Cloak.

He found Bern not by the fireplace but in the library. The old Sage was seated at his desk, bent over a cracked and yellow scroll. The library contained hundreds of such scrolls, rolled and tucked in the honeycombed alcoves along the walls. Several lay unattended on the desk, but none on the floor. Bern had too much respect for knowledge to allow that.

The Sage looked up as Kotyr entered. He frowned at the cloak in Kotyr’s hands. “We cannot accept payment for our duty.”

“Yet it’d be rude to deny their offerings,” Kotyr countered.

Bern considered for a moment. “Perhaps it would be,” he said with a smile, and Kotyr grinned and handed to him his cloak. Together they ventured into the cold of the Evergarden.

Above, the sun shone weakly from a blue sky dappled with feathery clouds. The grass rippled in the wind as they looked out to the Sea.

“There is something on your mind,” said Bern.

He did not ask the question directly, so Kotyr was not bound to answer by his oath of truth. For some time he sat quietly, listening to the sea waves breaking against the shore below, watching the gulls coast on the buffeting winds and dive arrow-like into the blue water. He marked one cloud, dark with storm, squatting over the emerald isles near the horizon.

“I have been thinking of what you spoke the other night,” he said eventually. “Of letting go.”

“What is it you cannot let go?”

“The pilgrims I see each morning,” Kotyr began. “Their children will be born into their lives. A Lord, or a Lady, or a simple shepherd. It makes me wonder. What role one’s blood plays in one’s nature.”

“Ah,” said Bern. “You think of your father.”

“I know nothing of him,” said Kotyr quietly.

“It is true that our nature and duty owe something to our blood,” spoke Bern. “But it is not birth alone that makes a person who he is. There are other influences—your teachers, your friends, and, if one is too quick to act on impulses, your past lives.”

Kotyr smiled. “Well said. I’m grateful for the influences I have. And their wisdom.” He was careful not to lie, yet he couldn’t help but wonder about his father.

Bern seemed about to reply when a cry sounded from behind, where the Lifetree stood.

Kotyr turned, frowning. If there was anyone there, they were hidden behind the Lifetree. “That sounded like—”

“Help!” cried the voice again. “I need a Sage!”

On the other side of the Lifetree sat three figures.

Their robes were black as the dead of night and their hoods cast long black shadows in which Kotyr saw gleaming eyes. He couldn’t guess their ages or their purpose.

Kotyr looked about sharply. He couldn’t tell how the strangers had arrived without being seen or heard. He gripped his short staff own tightly.

The figure on the left turned his gaze to Bern. “Are you a Sage?” he asked hoarsely.

“I am. Though I would know the names of those who come to the Lifetree,” Bern said cautiously. He took a step forward, staff in hand.

The man threw his hood back. His hair was black and greasy, dark curls hanging to his shoulders, and his expression was grim. His face was unmarked but for a white scar that streaked through a milky eye.

Kotyr knew his face. So did everyone in Hellendor.

Ifaldur. The Godbreaker, the only man to make the Emperor bleed. In return, Benedor had spared his life.

“I am Ifaldur,” came the reply. “With me are my father and daughter, but I fear the story of our coming must wait a little while longer. My father is running out of time.”

He pulled away his father’s hood.

Ipiru was the oldest man Kotyr had ever seen. He looked as though he were already dead. Only his face and hands were exposed, and both were decrepit, cratered with old scars and new cuts that still bled. He let out a whimper that rang of death.

“Every moment he draws nearer to the Gate,” said Ifaldur. “Please save him.”

“Such things are best saved for the time of dusk,” said Bern.

“He may not have until then.”

The old man seemed to speak then, quietly and with evident strain. Incoherent sounds tumbled from his dry, cracked lips.

Then his eyes sprang open. White eyes, without pupils. If they saw anything, it was certainly not of this realm.

“I hear them,” he croaked. “The whispers of the damned.” Those misty eyes rolled upwards and he let out a mangled screech as he convulsed. His back arched unnaturally as he seized with hellish spasms.

Kotyr stumbled back in fear. “What’s wrong with him?” he whispered.

Ifaldur’s gaze snapped to his, and Kotyr tried to hide his fear as he stared into the single, piercing gray eye. Kotyr wondered at the spark in his eye. Was it recognition?

“He has lived too long,” said the Godbreaker. “His mind is not his own. Help him, please,” he said as he turned back to Bern. “Let him live another life.”

“I will do what I can,” said Bern.

Ipiru’s seizure had lapsed, and he now whimpered pitifully, his hands over his face. Bern bent over his figure and gently wiped his tears. “Ilofilen nir alina aetheron,” he said, a whispered blessing. “Go in peace.”

Ipiru calmed at his words. He lay quietly now. Blood dripped from a gash in his hand to stain the grass.

Bern spoke the Words of Reaping, a thousand words layered in eerie resonance. His muscles jumped as he fought to control his Staff as it shook. The runes embossed in the wood began to glow, and swirls of brilliant white mist enshrouded both him and Ipiru.

“Thank you,” whispered a faint voice, finally at peace.

The glowing mist—Ipiru’s soul—drifted to the great Tree and circled it once, twice, three times, before it arced up into the canopy. Then Ipiru’s body burned to golden ash and blew away, scattered to the winds.

Ifaldur clasped his hands. His shoulders sagged, perhaps with relief, perhaps with grief.

Beside him, the last figure had finally removed her hood. She was a girl about Kotyr’s age, her face was framed by a curtain of shoulder-length dark ringlets. She looked to him suddenly, and he saw her pupils were a strange shade of lilac, piercing yet captivating. He offered her a smile. She didn’t return it.

Bern rose slowly. He gazed up at the Tree until a fresh golden fruit bloomed on a distant, high bough. Then he sighed in relief. “It is done.”

Ifaldur let fall a single tear. “I am in your debt, Sage. Thank you.”

“You owe no debt. It was my duty,” said Bern. “He was in much pain by the end. Why did he wait this long?”

“We had no choice.” He looked west. “A dragon comes.”

“A dragon?” asked Kotyr. He thought again of his dream and suppressed a shiver of fear.

“What you speak of is not possible,” said Bern.

“Do not deny that which I saw, with my own eyes.” He pointed to the emerald isles, to the dark shape Kotyr had taken for a storm cloud. “Can you not see it? There, above Erildor.”

Kotyr glanced at Bern. The old Sage looked troubled. “How can this be?” he asked quietly.

“I know not. But that thing killed my wife. And countless others. I will slay it. This I vow.” Ifaldur’s eyes flashed with a deep anger that left little doubt as to his reputation. The girl laid a hand on his arm and he relaxed, the fire waning from his gaze. “The dragon will come here, soon. In ten days. Perhaps sooner. We must leave. You would be wise to do the same.”

“Where will you go?” asked Bern.

“Feriador. Relios is King still, is he not? He has the Shards.”

The Shards. The broken blade Verrin used to kill the Emperor. His father Relios had taken them when the Empire fell, his only memento of his son’s life.

“Rest first,” advised Bern. “Rest, and dine with us. I would gladly hear your tale.”

Ifaldur looked to Kotyr again, a calculating glint in his eye. “I would gladly tell it,” he said.

## The Godbreaker’s Tale

“Perhaps you’ve heard of me. Or at least of my deeds.”

They were seated about the oak table in the Temple’s dining hall, waiting for Ifaldur’s tale. He had washed, if not rested, and his dark hair was curled with fresh oil, his beard neat and cropped close. He looked almost regal, even with the dark circles underscoring his eyes.

“I will clear the air now,” Ifaldur continued. “I did make the Emperor bleed. I did not break him, by any means, yet it is true he granted me my life as a boon. Do you have any questions?”

“How did you get here?” Kotyr asked, since he couldn’t help himself. He tried to ignore Bern’s look of disapproval.

Ifaldur turned his gaze to him. “And your name?”

“I’m called Kotyr.”

“Well met. Would that it were under better circumstances.” Ifaldur considered him, his eyes darting to the white of his robe. “Are you a Sage, Kotyr?”

“I am.”

“I see. Perhaps you could have reaped my father’s soul.”

“Perhaps. But I’ve only just become a Sage. Better that Bern did it.”

“As you say. But you cannot hide forever from your duty. Someday the world may ask you to step up.”

“And when it does, I will.” Something in Ifaldur’s tone rankled Kotyr.

“No doubt,” said the Godbreaker. He gestured to the girl at his side. “My daughter, Isa.”

Isa bowed her head. Her hair was dark and shiny, the dirt washed from her face. She still did not smile.

Ifaldur drew a deep breath. “My father was old, as you no doubt saw. Old enough that a moon ago, he decided he wished to Pass. But Qirin advised we wait, and though he chose not to share his reasons, we trusted his wisdom.”

He paused as the doors swung open. Tilia entered, carrying plates laden with their meal, and Kotyr rose quickly to help her pass them out.

“I’m sorry for the interruption,” she said to Ifaldur.

“There is nothing to apologize for,” he replied smoothly. “Please, do join us.”

“If you insist. I am glad to hear your father went in peace.”

“Thank you. What is your name?”

“Tilia,” she said.

“Tilia,” Ifaldur echoed. “You look like someone I once knew.” He caught her gaze and held it. “Where are you from?”

A moment’s silence. “Perhaps that story can wait,” said Bern.

Tilia nodded. She smiled at Kotyr, then turned to leave.

“Will you not stay?” Kotyr asked.

“Yes, do stay,” said Ifaldur.

Tilia hesitated, glancing at Bern. “Of course.” She took a seat beside Kotyr.

Ifaldur leaned over his plate, examining the food. Bread, beans, and a curry of fresh vegetables. He picked up the knife at set beside it. “I should have known a Sage would not eat meat.” Yet he ate anyway, as did the others.

Ifaldur finished his meal first. He pushed away his plate and drank from his cup. “A moon passed,” he continued. “Last night we returned to Erildor’s Lifetree, so my father could pass. It was a mistake. The dragon waited there.

“It was monstrous. White and scaled and winged. It was coiled around the Lifetree, waiting for us. There were perhaps twenty of us, who came in pilgrimage to die to the Tree. Qirin was there already.” He paused. “I will not forget the sight easily. He was floating ten span above the ground, his arms and legs splayed. Then he drifted toward the Tree, and in its trunk a black void opened.”

He looked at Bern as he said the last, as if expecting a reaction. When the old Sage did not grant him one, he continued.

But I averted my eyes and covered my daughters’ as well.” He paused again to lay a hand on his daughter’s shoulder. “I could not save my wife. She looked into that beast’s eyes, as did the others.

“No doubt you have heard the saying. Death waits, in the eyes of a dragon. It is true, what they say. All who looked into that beast’s eyes went the same way as Qirin. Dozens of them, twisted and floating, pulled into the Lifetree.”

“And you?” prompted Bern. “How did you find yourselves here?”

“We followed,” said Ifaldur. “We followed the bodies into the Tree. I could not see where they went. I could not see anything of that dark path. It was blind luck that led us here.”

“Luck could not help you navigate that path,” said Bern.

“Yet luck it was,” said Ifaldur sharply.

Bern held his gaze, but decided against pressing the point. “So that brings you here,” he said. “What will you do next?”

Tigers.” He twirled the knife again. “To hunt an animal, you must deeply understand its nature. Its wants, its needs. It is an intimate art.”

He leaned forward. “Do you know know what a dragon wants? What one needs?”

“Death,” whispered Kotyr.

“Death. And I will find it where I find life.” He looked out to the Garden. “Perhaps it will come here next. Perhaps another Lifetree. But I will be ready for it, when it comes.”

“You have no plan,” said Bern. He held up his hand when Ifaldur made to argue. “I merely state a fact. I do not fault you for it, I only offer my help.”

“How?” asked Ifaldur.

“Let me send a missive to King Gorin, to ask his aid. You would have a powerful ally in Konai.”

The Godbreaker considered. “Wise counsel,” he said.

“Let Gorin hear what you have told me, and we will think together on a plan. You are not alone in this, Ifaldur.”

“Not yet, it would seem. Thank you, Sage.”

Bern inclined his head. “Stay the night here. You will be safe in my Temple.”

“I have no need of being kept safe,” said Ifaldur, though he made no further argument. He rose to leave, then looked back at Bern. “I may have need of you again. If I call, will you answer?”

“If my duty allows it,” said the old Sage.
